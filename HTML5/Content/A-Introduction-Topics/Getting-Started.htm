Accessibility in web design

Accessibility is more than simply making things for people with disabilities to use. To ensure that web material is accessible to everyone regardless of ability - that something is universally accessible - we can make sure that we are creating material that is not easier for one demographic to use than another, whilst also making it adaptable to any format and any device.

Accessibility: accessible design versus universal design

Accessible design

Think of an apartment building with only a staircase inside and a manual front door with a step. It has rooms on the first floor, but they are limited in number. If you use a wheelchair, a leg brace, crutches or a cane, you would not be able to move up the stairs easily. Now, suppose you were to add on a stairlift for the staircase. While it might be easier to move upstairs and down, it would still be a process getting in and out of your wheelchair.

That is accessible design: it is material created largely for non-disabled people, adapted later on for people with disabilities, but it might still be difficult for some people with disabilities to use the designed technology well.

Universal design

Now, think of an apartment building with a sliding front door and an elevated concrete ramp leading up to it. It has an elevator near the entrance that can fit several people, with a wide opening that anybody can enter or exit. If you use a wheelchair or any other mobility device, you can enter and exit that elevator easily. What’s more, if you do not use a wheelchair or any other mobility device, you can still enter and exit that elevator easily.

That is universal design: everybody is on a level playing field and they can use the same material with ease. There is no separate technology for disabled people and no separate technology for non-disabled people; disabled people can use the same technology non-disabled people can.

Section 508 states that accessibility means “developing solutions to meet the needs of all users, with and without disabilities” (“Universal Design and Accessibility”). There is no necessity for things to be adapted later on; accessibility to all people is kept in mind from the creation onwards.

Accessibility in web design

Digital material on the web can also be designed to be accessible for everyone. It can be used on any device even if it is different from most devices. According to HTML And CSS: Design and Build Websites by Jon Duckett, web material is now being made more accessible to people who use a variety of digital devices “including desktop computers, laptops, tablets and mobile phones” (Duckett 7). One may choose to use a certain device as they are most comfortable using that particular device rather than something else.

Alternative text is useful for more than one situation. For example, if you are blind, it can give you a description of what the image is. However, that is not a blanket case.

Screen readers

A screen reader reads the text on a webpage out loud. Often used by blind or low-vision people - although people with learning disabilities or people who have trouble reading may use them too (“Common misconceptions about screen readers”), they can also read out alt text to people who might rely on it. Some forms of web content, including Adobe Acrobat Reader, come with screen readers (“Accessibility features”).

Visual hierarchy and contrast

Accessibility does not have to be solely for people with disabilities. If a web page has a good visual hierarchy in how it presents its text, and contrasts different modes of material well against each other, it is accessible. If the most important material is made to stand out effectively - so it can be accessed easily - it can be useful, but it should be used with care: “...if too many aspects are competing for your attention, it can be hard to find the key messages” (Duckett 468).
